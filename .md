# Intelligence Desk Assistant — Al Jazeera Arabic
## Implementation Proposal

---

## The Problem

Al Jazeera Arabic's editorial desk receives hundreds of wire dispatches every day through iNews — Reuters, AP, AFP, BBC, Fars News, and others. The feeds are verified. The sourcing is credible. The problem is volume, and what happens when credible sources disagree.

A story breaks. Within fifteen minutes:

- Fars News, citing Iran's Foreign Minister, reports nuclear negotiations have ended
- Reuters, citing a US State Department official, says talks are still ongoing
- AFP reports twelve killed in a strike near Rafah
- Hamas's Health Ministry, also via wire, puts the figure at nineteen

A desk editor has to catch all of that simultaneously, across dozens of parallel dispatches, and decide which number to run and how to frame the disagreement — while the next wave of dispatches is already arriving.

The goal of this system is straightforward: help editors see what matters faster, never let a factual conflict between agencies slip through unnoticed, and give journalists a structured starting point when they need to write quickly.

---

## What the System Does

Three things, in order of editorial priority:

### 1. Ranks incoming dispatches by urgency

Not every wire item is equal. A report of a missile strike landing in a populated area is more urgent than a follow-up diplomatic statement. The system reads dispatches as they come through iNews and scores them based on what's actually in them — severity of the reported event, how many agencies are independently covering it, and whether it represents a new development or a continuation of something already on the editor's radar.

The score is not a decision. It's a triage layer. It tells the editor: *this cluster of dispatches deserves your attention right now.*

### 2. Flags when agencies contradict each other

This is where the system earns its place. When two dispatches cover the same event but report different facts, the system surfaces the conflict immediately, side by side:

**Casualty discrepancy**
> AFP: *"12 killed in airstrike near Rafah crossing"*
> Hamas Health Ministry (via Al Jazeera feed): *"19 martyrs confirmed in the same strike"*

**Status conflict**
> Fars News (quoting Iranian FM): *"Negotiations with the US have definitively ended"*
> Reuters (quoting US official): *"Trump says talks are still ongoing and productive"*

**Outcome conflict**
> IDF statement: *"All incoming missiles were intercepted"*
> Iranian state media: *"Several missiles reached their targets"*

Each flag names both sources, shows both claims, and classifies what kind of disagreement it is — numbers, status, attribution, or outcome. The journalist decides how to report it. The system makes sure it doesn't get missed during a fast-moving shift.

One important point: the system only flags items it can confidently match to the same real-world event. "AFP reports 12 killed in Rafah" and "Reuters reports 7 killed in Khan Yunis" are two separate incidents, not a contradiction. The matching is based on location, timing, and the actors involved.

### 3. Generates a structured first draft on request

When an editor decides a cluster of dispatches warrants a full brief, they can request a draft situation report. The system pulls from all relevant agency dispatches in the window, attributes each claim to its source explicitly, and marks any known discrepancies inline — flagging them for the journalist rather than resolving them.

The draft is a working document, not a publishable story. The journalist shapes it, adds context, verifies where needed, and publishes under their own byline. The system writes a first pass in Arabic and English simultaneously.

---

## How It Sits Alongside iNews

iNews stays exactly as it is. This system reads from the same feed — it doesn't replace it, doesn't modify how dispatches arrive, and doesn't touch the existing editorial workflow.

In practice, an editor on shift would have their normal iNews view on one side and a priority feed on the other: a ranked, real-time view of what the system considers the most significant active stories, with contradiction flags appearing as they're detected. Clicking a flag opens the two conflicting dispatches side by side.

The editorial judgment is always the journalist's. The system surfaces; it never decides.

---

## What This Is Not

To be direct about scope:

- **Not an autonomous publisher.** Nothing is drafted, filed, or broadcast without a journalist in the loop.
- **Not a replacement for iNews.** It's additive — another lens on the same feeds.
- **Not a source verification layer.** The assumption, which is Al Jazeera's existing assumption, is that iNews feeds are agency-verified. This system works downstream of that verification, not instead of it.
- **Not a translation tool.** Al Jazeera's journalists and translators handle language. The system produces working drafts in both Arabic and English, but those are inputs to the editorial process, not outputs from it.

---

## How It Would Roll Out

**Phase 1 — Surface and rank (3–4 months)**
Connect to the iNews feed. Build the urgency scoring. Display a ranked priority view alongside the normal dispatch flow. No AI-generated content in this phase — just better ordering and real-time highlighting of high-priority developments. Editors use it as a second screen. This phase is low risk and immediately useful.

**Phase 2 — Contradiction detection (2–3 months)**
Add the cross-agency conflict flagging. This is the most editorially sensitive feature and needs calibration. The first 60 days would involve supervised use where editors mark false positives — cases where the system flagged a "contradiction" that was actually just two agencies covering different aspects of the same story. That feedback sharpens the detection.

**Phase 3 — Draft generation (2–3 months)**
Add the AI-generated situation report drafts, triggered on demand by the editor. Not automatic, not on a timer — a journalist requests a draft when they want one. The draft is reviewed, edited, and published as their own work.

---

## What We Need to Get Started

Two things, both on the Al Jazeera side:

**iNews API access.** Read access to the wire feed in a structured format — ideally the same data iNews already displays, just available programmatically. This is the single technical dependency that the editorial technology team would need to facilitate.

**An editorial policy decision on AI drafts.** Specifically: what the draft may and may not be used for, and how attribution is handled if a published piece started from a system-generated first draft. This is an editorial governance question, not a technical one, and it should be answered before Phase 3 begins.

---

## Why Now

The volume of wire content covering the Middle East has increased significantly over the last two years. The pace of developments — particularly across multiple active theaters simultaneously — means editors are being asked to track more, faster, with the same team size. The tools haven't kept up.

This system doesn't reduce the journalist's role. It removes the parts of the job that don't require journalism: scanning for what matters, catching when agencies disagree, writing the first structural skeleton of a brief. What remains — judgment, sourcing, framing, accountability — stays entirely with the editorial team.
